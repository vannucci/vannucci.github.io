"Hi, my name is Mike, and I panic when I start projects"

"Hello, Mike, and welcome to the Dev Team. We're so glad you joined us and are willing to share."

![7 Stages of Grief](https://usercontent2.hubstatic.com/13512733_f520.jpg)

(Why does this image summarize my work process? I feel attacked.)

 Sometimes my boss will go on JIRA and start assigning tickets that he thinks are important to the devs. Senior devs will get big projects (Global Authorization refactor) and Junior devs will get small projects (hook up this button to an API endpoint). Being a Junior dev, I typically get projects which are focused on wiring up our frontend with the server that manages it all.

 I've never had a project which I've had to pass on to someone else, someone more capable. Sure I've asked for help or guidance, and once in a while I've cowrote some some code alongside a senior developer. But by and large I get my tickets, I run them down, solve and test them, and then they're reviewed and put into production*.

 That star marks a lie. Not a huge lie, but a lie all the same. The lie is that it's _that easy_. I waltz through the steps of my own problem solving process until the PR (pull request) is merged.

 Here is a better view of how I solve problems:

 1. Read the problem and hate the problem writer
 1. Strip away the impossible dreams of the problem writer to the meager doable work
 1. Devise an oversimplified and deceptively simple abstract solution which I will chase
 1. Slowly chip away at the problem while berating myself for slowness
 1. Devise a partial solution and resign myself to ignominy and never writing software again
 1. Experience a minor breakthrough or insight which unlocks the rest of the work
 1. Solicit senior developer help, have your dreams crushed when all your mistakes are pointed out

 (Oh you thought step 7 would be Acceptance? NOOB.) 

 Congratulations, you're now a software developer. Wait, you thought there was a stage where you triumphantly completed the work, submitted a PR and it just worked?

 Oh you're new here. Dude you are in for a surprise.
