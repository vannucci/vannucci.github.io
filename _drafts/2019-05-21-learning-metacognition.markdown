# Introduction
In my past life I was a teacher of physics to high school students. I attended the Rutgers Graduate School of Education in order to get my masters in education and my teaching license. During that time I learned what I feel is one of the most powerful ideas I have ever heard of: metacognition. This term means "thinking about thinking", or reflecting on the way your own brain thinks about things or processes things. It may seem like a ten dollar word for a silly concept, but to anyone learning something new (and this can be defined VERY broadly) thinking about your own thought processes is essential. You ask yourself what models you use for the world, how it is that you interpret new information, and what past knowledge guides what how you store or incorporate new things.

When I read Andy Matuschak's article [1] it finally occurred to me (or re-occurred to me) that I have been treating books and reading non-fiction the same way I've treated fiction books. I read them as entertainment, letting the words pass by my eyes and maybe taking a few notes to "read again later" in my email. I've never thought about my own thinking and reading processing towards technical material, and I've never (until very recently) thought about my own thinking process when it came to the work of software development.

My goal in this next few posts on this topic of metacognition is to explore what it means to "think about thinking" in three specific instances related to the work of software development. The first is the metacognition of _reading and learning_ about computer science and software development. The second will be the metacognition of _application and practice_ of software development. Next I'll talk about the metacognition of _emotions_ surrounding doing difficult problems and interacting with peers. Lastly, I'll talk about metacognition with respect to _public work and speaking_ (this last one is from my experiences teaching high school students, NOT doing software presentations, yet).

# Define Metacognition and Metacognitive Skills (for Reading and Learning)
Has it ever occurred to you that you've just skimmed over something that you wanted to read? You read the first few words, but then you just sort of skipped to beginning of each, and finally you just skipped to the end and said, "Maybe they wrote a tl;dr summary..." and drew your conclusions from there?

Thinking about the fact that you glazed over something is metacognition. You thought about your own thinking/action of barely reading, the fact that you were distracted with another thought, and now you've come around to thinking "Oh I understood this article" or "Well this isn't useful" or as I do "I'll save this for later" and then never come back to it. Realizing that this latest thought is a form of self-deception _is itself metacognition!!!_.

So being aware of your own thoughts and what their meaning is to you is what we call metacognition. It's an essential skill to learn any new thing, since you are the only person who can make a call as to how your brain has processed and digested new things.
# A General Template or Guideline To A Good Metacognitive Skill (for Reading and Learning)
So how can we use this for our reading, or for our goal of learning well from books? First we must recognize that just skimming a book does not lead to learning. At all. Don't believe the movies they are fiction. In order to learn we must _engage_ what we learn and practice it.

Now in programming this is quite easy, you might say. Fire up a REPL, and just type out whatever the book gives as examples, right?

```
def hello_world():
    print('Hello, world!')
```

This is a step in the right direction, yes. But if you went through a tutorial or a book and just typed out by reading and rote every example you read, you would learn _better_ but not completely.
