I'm heading in to my second year as a full time professional software developer. The "official" one year anniversary will be around May 2019, and I can confidently say I have learned a lot in this time. Most of my learning is around what is considered my day to day work, modifying our company's app (AngularJS), debugging and using JIRA, good practices around git and version control, as well as truly understanding common pitfalls in software writing (asynchronous processes, global scoping, clear comments and useful API documentation).

Where I feel I am lacking are in the fundamentals of computer science. While I understand the basics of what recursion is and OOP, knowing when and how to apply those techniques is where I often fall short. My goal for this year, 2019, is to fully read Steven Skiena's book "The Algorithm Design Manual" as well as Jeremy Kun's "A Programmer's Introduction To Mathematics". As well, I am applying to the Georgia Tech OMSCS program and hope to attend in the Fall of 2019.

As a former educator, my mind often wanders back to the most fundamental of epistemological questions, "How do I know what it is I think I know?" After all I tried to impress on my students that while a teacher can _teach_, the burden of proving understanding lies with the student. So, in reading these books and wanting to understand and know the topic, I know that working on this, the burden of proof of understanding, is the main thing any self-learner should focus on.

So in this entry I want to share with you a few ideas about this question, as well as some common techniques I am trying to employ. This is not meant as a "Guide To Perfectly Learn Anything, Instantly!" but more as a "Here are ways we could be doing things".

## Fundamental Concepts

To begin with, let's talk about the basic concepts behind effective learning of new things.

1. Recall is King -- If you cannot recall, or give from memory something which you have learned, or you find yourself saying "Oh yeah I know it when I read it" then you haven't actually learned a concept or topic. You may deceive yourself that "Memorization doesn't matter" or "I know it when I see it" or whatever you want to lie to yourself about, but the ability to actively recall something when it's needed is what actual learning is.

1. Spaced Repetition -- Even just recalling an idea _once_ won't cut it. Often it will take dozens and dozens of recalls and in different ways to really get something to sink in. It's why I would often tell my students that, while you may think I'm smart with physics, the reality is I have been doing it for many, many years to the point where recall is quite automatic to me.
